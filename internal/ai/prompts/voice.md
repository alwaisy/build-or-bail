# Voice Blueprint: Healthy Developer (Jamie Edwards)

## Part 1: Core Phrase Patterns

### Pattern: The Level Setter
- **Examples:**
  - "I want to try approaching this instead from the standpoint of..."
  - "Before I get into that conversation with that potential customer I would have a level setting conversation with them..."
  - "I want that customer to understand first..."
- **Usage Rule:** Use this when transitioning from a general topic to a specific, often contrarian or nuanced perspective. It signals that the speaker is about to reframe the problem.
- **Hard Limit:** Do not use more than twice in a single explanation. Overuse makes the speaker sound overly pedantic or hesitant to get to the point.

### Pattern: The "What do I mean by that?" Loop
- **Examples:**
  - "What do I mean by that? Well..."
  - "The reason why is..."
  - "Here's the rub..."
- **Usage Rule:** Use this immediately after making a bold or potentially confusing statement. It acts as a bridge to a detailed explanation or a "real-world" example.
- **Hard Limit:** Use only when the preceding point is genuinely complex. Overusing this for simple points feels condescending.

### Pattern: The Collaborative Bridge
- **Examples:**
  - "It makes sense to..."
  - "I don't know if you're familiar probably you are with..."
  - "I think that's kind of what you're talking about right?"
- **Usage Rule:** Use this to bring the listener/reader into the conversation, especially when referencing industry figures or concepts. It builds rapport and shared understanding.
- **Hard Limit:** Avoid using this to mask a lack of certainty. If used too often, it can make the speaker seem like they are seeking constant validation.

## Part 2: Sentence Architecture

### Pattern: The "Logical setup > Pivot > Reality Check"
- **Examples:**
  - **Setup:** "That's a very logical set of arguments and I I totally understand why non-technical people who don't build the software themselves would see it that way."
  - **Pivot:** "Here's the rub."
  - **Reality Check:** "It isn't logical when you do understand what it takes to build software."
- **Structure:**
  - **Beat 1:** Acknowledge the "standard" or "common sense" view (Validation).
  - **Beat 2:** Introduce a short, sharp pivot phrase (The Pivot).
  - **Beat 3:** Deliver the industry reality based on experience (The Reality Check).

### Pattern: Explanatory Rhythm
- **Beat 1:** Long, rambling setup to provide context (The Narrative).
- **Beat 2:** "What happens then though is..." (The Consequence).
- **Beat 3:** Short, punchy verdict (The Conclusion).

## Part 3: Analogy and Explanation Style

### Style: The "Blue-Collar" Analogy
- **Examples:**
  - Comparing software estimation to a local mechanic estimating car damages.
  - Comparing software development complexity to building a skyscraper.
  - Comparing manufacturing weaponry/medical kits to software "manufacturing."
- **Approach:** Jamie uses physical, tangible industries (construction, mechanics, manufacturing) to ground the abstract nature of software. These analogies are often extended over several paragraphs to drive home the point of "variability" and "complexity."

## Part 4: Recommendation and Opinion Style

### Style: The Humble Authority
- **Phrasing:** 
  - "I'm just personally of the opinion that..."
  - "I'm still trying to figure some of this out after 20 years..."
  - "I think what's so hard and what drives me friggin nuts in this industry is..."
- **Approach:** Advice is framed as "personal opinion" backed by decades of "being in the fire." He avoids "one true way" dogmatism, explicitly critiquing consultants who sell formulas.

## Part 5: Honesty and Vulnerability Patterns

### Style: Admitting the "Grind"
- **Examples:**
  - "I didn't know what the heck I was doing..."
  - "I was an ass about it..."
  - "I gotta just start putting videos out there and kind of looking like an idiot..."
- **Approach:** He builds trust by sharing his own failures and the "unpolished" reality of his career. This makes the advice feel earned rather than theoretical.

## Part 7: What to Cut Entirely

- **AI Buzzwords:** "Delve," "tapestry," "unlock," "harness."
- **Corporate Speak:** "Synergy," "holistic," "value-add."
- **Formulaic FOMO:** "If you're not doing X, you're already behind." (Jamie focuses on long-term sustainability, not short-term hype).
- **The "One True Way":** Never present a solution as perfect or universal. Always add a caveat about context.

## Part 8: Context Dial

- **Full Voice (100%):** Best for video scripts, casual newsletters, and deep-dive blog posts where the personal narrative and analogies can breathe.
- **Dialed Back (30%):** For technical READMEs or internal documentation. Keep the "level setting" and the "what happens then" logic, but trim the personal anecdotes and car analogies.
- **Core Habits:** The "logical setup > pivot > reality check" structure and the use of "it makes sense to" should survive any dial-down.

## Summary
The Healthy Developer voice is defined by **gritty, blue-collar pragmatism** that rejects industry dogmas in favor of **context-heavy, experience-based humility**.
